Core Research Ideas

Quantitative data analysis generally aims to do one of three things: (1) describe, (2) predict, or (3) figure out what’s causing what. The steps below focus on what we can do to examine that third aim -- causation -- when we can’t conduct a true experiment (randomly assign people to one group/condition vs. another). For people relatively new to research, textbooks and blogs about how to do research contain overwhelming amounts of information to process and synthesize. The following documents boil the research process down to the essentials. Part 1 below presents a step-by-step guide to thinking about research methodology and designing an analysis. Part II provides a guide to carrying out the actual statistical anlayses.

Part I: Methodological Framework

1. Frame research question as: Examine whether a change in one variable (the independent variable) causes a change in another variable (the dependent variable) because…(describe the reason/theory).

  • Terminology:
  • Independent variable (IV)[a]: the variable doing the causing.
  • Dependent variable (DV)[b]: the variable being affected.
  • Most of the time, people want to know what’s causing what. That said, you might be interested in a non-causal relationship: Simply, does a change in the IV relate to a change in the DV for a particular reason? But this doc will focus on analyzing causal questions. It’s the (much) harder question to analyze, so pulling things back to non-causal questions will be easy after thinking about causality.
  • We/science use three things to infer that an IV causes a DV (and all of the guidelines in this doc and the statistics/analysis doc are aimed at examining one of these three points):
  • The two variables need to appear related (correlated).
  • The two variables need to appear correctly ordered in time: The observed variation in the IV needs to occur before the observed variation in the DV.
  • Lack of plausible alterntavie explanations for the relationship between the IV and DV; i.e., lack of explanations for the relationship other than changes in the IV caused changes in the DV.        

Example: People who take more naps (IV) are less likely to die of heart problems (DV) because sleep heals the heart.

2. Measure the IV and DV.

  • Think about different ways to measure/quantify the IV and DV.
  • How accurately are you able to measure what you want to measure? (When we measure a variable well enough, the measure is “valid” or has “validity.”)

3. Are the IV and DV related? Do changes in the IV covary/correlate with changes in the DV strongly and consistently enough to trust that they are really related?

  • Use data visualization and regression/p-values to examine this question. See “Analyzing Data” doc’s first section “Analyze the relationship bewteen IV and DV in isolation.”
  • Key point from philosophy of science: The visualization and statistical analysis of the relationship between the IV and DV will provide some evidence either for or against the existence of a relationship. These tools will never never ever prove that there’s a relationship or prove that there isn’t a relationship. Science never proves anything. Science accumulates evidence/clues. Each analysis of data is a piece of evidence to think about in conjunction with other evidence and common sense.

4. If the IV and DV appear related, think about plausible alternative explanations for their relatedness (i.e., reasons other than the IV caused the DV). Plausible is the key word here. Coming up with implausible altenrnative explanations is being a naysayer and isn’t constructive. To come up with alternative explanations:

  • Think about whether it’s plausble that the DV caused the IV.
  • Think about whether it’s plausible that another “third” variable (a) occurs in time before the IV and the DV, (b) varies systematically (i.e., covaries/correlates) with the IV and (c) caused changes in the DV. These types of variables are called “confounds.” Brainstorm and create a list of plausible confounds (skip unlikely ones).
  • Though counfounds correlate with the IV, they should not be affected/caused by the IV. A variable that the IV affects, which in turn affects the DV, is a mediator, not a confound. A mediator shows the process by which the IV affects the DV.
  • Given this, a confound generally does not refer to something that occurs after the IV.  

Example:

  • In our napping-deaths example, is it possible that the DV caused the IV? No -- we can rule out this alternative explanation. Death can’t cause napping. Not logical. Wrong temporal order. (If our DV were “heart health” instead of death, then we would have to worry about whether the DV caused the IV. It’s possible that poor heart health--say, in the forn of higher heart rate--interferes with napping and that’s why we see the inverse IV-DV relationship, rather than because more napping improves heart health).
  • In our example, is it possible that confouding variables exist?--that a variable both relates to napping and causes deaths from heart disease? Maybe heavy time pressure in a person’s job? The degree of job time pressure may be correlated with napping (more time pressure → less napping) and an increase in time pressure may increase likelihood of dying from heart problems. If this these two things are true, then they create an alternative explanation for why more napping is correlated with fewer deaths from heart problems: these two variables may be correlated only because they are both byproducts of having a job with lots of time pressure. Note that the confound--a job’s time pressure--is something that occurs and is known prior to the the napping IV.

5. Attempt to rule out plausible alternative explanations for the relationship between the IV and DV by controlling for counfounds.

  • Measure each plausible confounding variable.
  • The measurement of the confound should use information only available before the measurment of the IV. 
  • If the measurment of the confound occurs after the measurement of the IV, it should only refer to information known prior to the IV.
  • Examine the relationship between the confound and the IV, and bewteen the counfound and the DV.
  • Use “Analyzing Data” doc’s first section “Analyze the relationship bewteen IV and DV in isolation” for steps to look at the relationship between two variables.
  • Does it seem correct that the suspected confound is related to both the IV and the DV?
  • Hold the confound constant while analyzing relationship between IV and DV:
  • By including confounding variable(s) in a regression and so statistically controlling for it. If the IV-DV relationship remains while statistically controlling for the confound(s), then it’s less likely the counfound(s) accounts for the IV-DV relatinship and in turn the causal relationship becomes more likely. See the “Analyzing Data” doc’s second section “Analyze the relationship bewteen IV and DV while controlling for potential confounds” for details.
  • By analyzing relationship between IV and DV at a particular level of the confound. If the relationship between IV and DV remains while holding the confound constant, the likelihood that the confound can account for the IV-DV relationship decreases and in turn the explanation that the IV causes the DV becomes more likely as an explanation for their correlation.
  • By doing an experiment or quasi-experiment (lots of nuances to doing experiments and quasi-experiments that can cover at another time).

Example:

  • Analyze the relationship between naps and deaths from heart problems only among those with one particular kind of job (which in effect holds the time pressure of one’s job constant). Or figure out a way to measure/quantify time pressure in one’s job and include this measure in a regression that analyzes the relationship between naps and death from heart problems.
  • The measure of a person’s job or time pressure in the job should refer to what we know about a person’s job prior to measuring the napping IV.

6. Explore possible moderator variables. These are variables that moderate/alter the relationship between the IV and DV.

  • Think about whether the IV might relate to the DV more or less strongly under certain circumstances, for certain people, etc. It’s almost alway the case that the strength of an IV-DV relationship will vary at different levels of another “moderating” variable.
  • We learn a alot about why an IV causes a DV when we figure out what variables moderate the IV-DV relationship (i.e., moderation analyses help us test and refine our theory about why the IV causes the DV).
  • Changes or levels of the moderator must bet set prior to variation in the IV. That way we eliminate the possiblity that the IV affected the moderator. We can then conclude that at different levesls of the moderator, the IV-DV relationship changes.
  • Even if the IV and DV aren’t related in general, it’s worth examining whether they may be related at a particular level of a moderator. There are many IVs that don’t relate to DVs on average but that do relate under certain circumstances. If you don’t analyze the IV-DV relationship under these particular circumstances you will falsly concude they are unrelated.

Example: Maybe naps improve deaths from heart problems particularly among people who don’t sleep well? If our theory about why IV causes DV is correct, then naps should relate more strongly to deaths from heart disease for those who sleep poorly at night and naps should relate more weakly to heart disease deaths for those those who sleep well.

Summary

  1. Frame research question as: Examine whether a change in one variable (the IV) causes a change in another variable (the DV) because…(describe the reason).
  2. Think about how best to measure the IV and DV.
  3. Examine whether the IV and DV are related using visualization and the p-value.
  4. If the IV and DV appear related, think about plausible alternative explanations for their relatedness. To come up with alternative explanations:
  1. Think about whether it’s plausble that the DV caused the IV.
  2. Think about confounds: variables that (1) vary systematically with the IV and (2) may cause changes in the DV.
  1. Attempt to rule out plausible alternative explanations by holding confounds constant while analyzing relationship between IV and DV.
  2. Explore possible moderators of the relationship between the IV and DV to learn about why the IV affects the DV.
